## Key Policy Provisions to Include

### Definition of Workplace Violence
Use a broad, enforceable definition:
- Physical assault or battery
- Threats of violence (verbal, written, electronic, gestured)
- Intimidation, bullying, or harassment creating fear of physical harm
- Stalking
- Property damage with intent to intimidate
- Brandishing or possession of weapons on company property
- Domestic violence that impacts the workplace

### Workplace Violence Categories (Type I-IV)
- **Type I -- Criminal Intent**: Perpetrator has no relationship to workplace (robbery, trespassing)
- **Type II -- Customer/Client**: Violence by someone receiving services
- **Type III -- Worker-on-Worker**: Current or former employee
- **Type IV -- Personal Relationship**: Domestic violence spillover to workplace

  - Concerning behavior changes
  - Knowledge of weapons on premises
  - Domestic situations that may affect workplace safety
  - Restraining orders that name the workplace or a coworker

### Response Levels
- **Level 1 -- Awareness**: Documented, monitored, no immediate action required
- **Level 2 -- Intervention**: TAT engaged, subject contacted, management action, possible EAP referral
- **Level 3 -- Protective Action**: Separation from workplace, restraining order pursuit, law enforcement involvement, enhanced security measures
- **Level 4 -- Imminent Threat**: 911, lockdown, active threat response, executive notification

### Restraining Orders and Protection Orders
- Employees shall notify HR or Security when a restraining order lists the workplace or a coworker
- Security shall:
  - Obtain copy of the order (with employee consent)
  - Brief relevant personnel (reception, security officers) on restricted individual
  - Adjust access controls if named individual has workplace access
  - Develop safety plan with protected employee
- [USER INPUT REQUIRED: State-specific employer obligations regarding workplace restraining orders]

### Domestic Violence Protocol
- EAP referral and support resources
- Workplace safety planning with affected employee
- Schedule and parking adjustments if needed
- Security awareness for reception and lobby staff
- Confidentiality protections for affected employee

### Termination and Separation Risk
- All involuntary terminations reviewed by Security for risk assessment
- High-risk terminations may include:
  - Security escort
  - Immediate credential deactivation
  - IT access termination
  - Personal property collection with supervision
  - Post-separation monitoring period
- Voluntary resignations flagged if preceded by concerning behavior

### Training
- All employees: Annual awareness training on recognizing and reporting warning signs
- Managers/supervisors: Additional training on intervention, documentation, and referral
- Security team: Behavioral threat assessment training
- Reception/front desk: De-escalation and threat recognition
- New hire orientation includes workplace violence prevention

## Common Legal Considerations

- **OSHA General Duty Clause**: Employers must provide a workplace free from recognized hazards, including violence
- **California SB 553** (effective July 2024): Requires written WVPP, training, violent incident log
- **State firearms laws**: Many states prohibit employers from banning firearms in employee personal vehicles in parking lots

- **NLRA considerations**: Policy language must not chill protected concerted activity
- **ADA/FMLA**: Threatening behavior linked to a disability requires interactive process before termination
- **Duty to Warn**: Some jurisdictions impose duty to warn identifiable victims of credible threats
- **Workers compensation**: Workplace violence injuries are generally compensable
- **Negligent hiring/retention**: Employer liability for failing to screen or act on known risks
